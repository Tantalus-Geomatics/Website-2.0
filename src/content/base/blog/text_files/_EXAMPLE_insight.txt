### Body

If you have recently received subdivision approval or a development permit in British Columbia, there is a good chance your municipality, lender, or engineer has mentioned a **Section 219 covenant**. For most property owners, this is a term that appears in a stack of legal documents without much explanation. This guide demystifies what a covenant is, why it is being required of you, and what role a British Columbia Land Surveyor (*BCLS*) plays in preparing and registering it.

## What Is a Section 219 Covenant?

A covenant registered under **Section 219 of the Land Title Act** is a legally binding agreement between a property owner and a second party — most commonly a municipality, the Province of British Columbia, or a utility provider — that restricts or requires specific uses of the land. Unlike a contract between two people, a Section 219 covenant is registered directly on the land title and binds not just the current owner but all future owners of the property.

[[image: covenant-plan-example.webp | float: right | width: 38 | caption: "Example of a registered Section 219 covenant plan showing the septic reserve boundary"]]

Common examples of what a Section 219 covenant can restrict or require include:

* Designating a specific area of the lot as an approved septic field reserve that cannot be built upon
* Prohibiting the construction of any building within a defined riparian setback from a watercourse
* Restricting the density or height of future buildings on a parcel
* Requiring the maintenance of a natural vegetation buffer along a property boundary

## Why Does a Municipality Require One?

Municipalities use Section 219 covenants as a development control tool. When a property owner applies to subdivide land, obtain a development permit, or seek a variance, the municipality may impose a covenant as a condition of approval. This allows the local authority to protect environmental features, infrastructure corridors, or neighbourhood character in perpetuity — regardless of how many times the property changes hands.

Your lender may also require a covenant search and review as part of their due diligence before advancing mortgage funds. An existing covenant that limits development potential can affect the appraised value of the property and, consequently, the amount a lender is willing to advance.

The Sea to Sky corridor sees a high volume of Section 219 covenants tied to septic reserves and riparian setbacks, given the prevalence of on-site sewage systems and watercourses throughout Squamish, Whistler, and the surrounding rural areas.

## What Does a BC Land Surveyor Do?

Preparing a Section 219 covenant requires a *BCLS* whenever the covenant area is defined by a legal boundary or a surveyed line rather than a simple administrative description. The *BCLS* will:

1. Research the parent parcel boundaries through the LTSA and confirm existing survey monuments
2. Survey and define the covenant area boundary in the field using RTK GNSS and Robotic Total Stations
3. Prepare a statutory **Covenant Plan** — a certified legal plan that shows the covenant boundary in relation to the property lines, ready for filing at the Land Title Office
4. Work with your conveyancing lawyer and the covenant holder (municipality or Province) to coordinate the registration sequence

The covenant plan is a legal instrument. Only a commissioned *BCLS* holds the statutory authority under the **Land Surveyors Act** to prepare and certify it.

## How Long Does It Take?

A straightforward covenant survey for a residential lot typically takes two to four weeks from engagement to registered plan, depending on current LTSA processing times and the complexity of the covenant boundary. For projects on a subdivision or development permit critical path, engaging your surveyor early is essential — covenant registration must often precede the final subdivision approval or building permit issuance.
