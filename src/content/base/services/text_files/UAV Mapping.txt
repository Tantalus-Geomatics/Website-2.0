### Steps
1. **Research**
The foundation of a safe and accurate UAS survey is exhaustive mission planning and research. The BCLS accesses the Land Title and Survey Authority of British Columbia (LTSA) to retrieve the registered subdivision plans and active title records that legally define your parcel, and consults municipal Geographical Information Systems to plan out the data acquisition strategy. Before any flight occurs, Transport Canada legally mandates that our pilots conduct a rigorous site survey under CAR 901.27. The BCLS consults resources like the National Research Council's Drone Site Selection Tool and NAV Drone to identify controlled airspace (Classes C, D, E), nearby aerodromes, and proximity to bystanders. We also review macro and micro weather conditions, NOTAMs, and select locations for setting physical ground control points.

2. **Field Work**
Upon mobilizing to your site in {{LOCATION_NAME}}, our survey crews must first conduct a meticulous physical search to recover the original legal survey monuments, ensuring the aerial data is perfectly tied to your legal boundaries. We then distribute Ground Control Points (GCPs) across the site, measuring them with RTK GNSS and Robotic Total Stations to guarantee absolute spatial accuracy. The physical terrain, local weather patterns (such as urban airflow or high-relief winds), and vegetative density directly impact the flight scope, required swath overlap, and time required to complete the survey. Utilizing our advanced UAS platforms, we execute precise, automated flight lines to capture overlapping high-resolution imagery and discrete-return LiDAR pulses. 

3. **Plan Preparation**

### FAQs section
**1. What is the difference between Aerial Photogrammetry and Aerial LiDAR?**
Photogrammetry uses overlapping, high-resolution photographs to calculate 3D measurements and create visual surface models; however, it generally only maps the top visible surface (the canopy or roof). LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) is an active sensor that emits laser pulses. These pulses can penetrate small gaps in dense vegetation to record multiple returns, allowing us to map the "bare earth" ground beneath a forest canopy.

**2. Why do I need a British Columbia Land Surveyor (BCLS) for drone mapping?**
Under the *Land Surveyors Act*, determining the location of any natural or artificial feature relative to a legal property boundary for the purpose of certifying it in writing is exclusively defined as the "practice of land surveying". Only a commissioned BCLS holds the statutory authority and professional liability insurance to legally map these features relative to your property lines.

**3. Can your drones fly anywhere?**
No. The airspace in Canada is strictly regulated by Transport Canada under CARs Part IX. Flying near airports, heliports, or within controlled airspace requires specific pilot certifications (Advanced Operations), properly registered drones, and explicit authorization from air navigation service providers (like NAV CANADA) prior to takeoff. 

**4. How accurate is drone surveying compared to traditional ground surveying?**
When executed by professional land surveyors, drone mapping is exceptionally accurate. However, aerial data is only as reliable as the ground control it is tied to. We achieve absolute survey-grade accuracy by physically placing ground control points (GCPs) and measuring them with RTK GNSS and Robotic Total Stations, ensuring the drone data seamlessly aligns with our rigid terrestrial measurements.

**5. Do you need a special permit to fly over people or out of sight?**
Yes. Under Transport Canada regulations, flying over bystanders or conducting Beyond Visual Line-of-Sight (BVLOS) operations carries significantly higher risk. These operations require the drone to have a manufacturer safety assurance declaration for that specific environment, and BVLOS flights legally require a Special Flight Operations Certificate (SFOC) approved by Transport Canada.
